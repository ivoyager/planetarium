# Spacecraft Trajectory & Ephemeris Data

Spacecraft trajectories (`tree_components/trajectory.gd`, built from `orbits.tsv` segments that are listed in `trajectories.tsv` and assigned to a craft in `spacecrafts.tsv`) are **patched-conic**: an ordered set of `IVOrbit` segments, each a conic about one gravitational primary, joined at sphere-of-influence (SOI) crossings. Heliocentric **cruise** legs are flagged `fix_gaps` and re-fitted at new-game to meet the **flyby** anchor legs (see *Gap closing* below). The data is generated by `tools/horizons_trajectory.py`.

## Authoritative source — NASA/JPL HORIZONS

All real spacecraft (and body) ephemeris data comes from the JPL Solar System Dynamics **HORIZONS** system — the same group that produces the DE planetary ephemerides. It is *the* primary source; **do not use third-party mirrors or aggregators.**

- Web app: https://ssd.jpl.nasa.gov/horizons/
- REST API (use this for the conversion pipeline): https://ssd.jpl.nasa.gov/api/horizons.api
- HORIZONS ingests the official reconstructed-trajectory **SPICE SPK kernels** from NASA **NAIF** (https://naif.jpl.nasa.gov/) — the underlying archival source and the right provenance citation. We query HORIZONS rather than raw SPK because HORIZONS performs the SPK→osculating-Keplerian-element conversion that `orbits.tsv` needs, with no SPICE-toolkit dependency.

## Targets (spacecraft are negative NAIF IDs)

| Craft | `COMMAND` | HORIZONS solution | Coverage |
|---|---|---|---|
| Voyager 1 | `-31` | `Voyager_1_ST+refit2022` (Jacobson, DE440) | 1977-09-05 → 2100 |
| Voyager 2 | `-32` | 2022 refit (Jacobson, DE440) | 1977-08-20 → 2100 |
| Pioneer 10 | `-23` | JPL Nav merged (PN10A–G, DE118) | 1972-03-03 → ~2002 tracking |
| Juno | `-61` | JPL reconstructed SPK (post-Centaur-separation) | 2011-08-05 → 2025-09 (Jupiter impact) |
| New Horizons | `-98` | NH nav concatenated SPK (recon. → 2019, pred. → 2050; plu060 Pluto recon, Brozovic & Jacobson) | 2006-01-19 → 2050 |

Juno is the one **capture** mission (the others fly by and escape): Earth → a multi-year heliocentric loop with an Earth gravity assist → Jupiter orbit insertion, then years of polar orbits. It uses the `visual_orbit` segment kind and the multi-revolution-cruise split in *Capture orbits and multi-revolution cruises* below.

New Horizons is the worked example of a **dwarf-planet flyby anchor**: Earth → Jupiter gravity assist → Pluto-Charon flyby → Kuiper Belt escape. Pluto anchors a flyby segment exactly like a giant planet would — any body modeled with a GM works (`PLANET_PLUTO`, GM 871 km³/s²), the segment is just tuned to Pluto's far smaller SOI (~3×10⁶ km, crossed in days at the ~14 km/s encounter speed): a ±2-day window vs the ±15-day giant-planet windows, with an explicit closest-approach sample epoch. The eccentricity there is ~2986 — a near-straight-line hyperbola, since Pluto's weak gravity barely deflects the pass; the open-conic `semi_parameter`/`time_periapsis` form carries it fine. The 2019 Arrokoth flyby is **not** modeled as an anchor: Arrokoth exists only as a massless asteroid row (no GM, no SOI) and barely perturbed the heliocentric escape, so the post-Pluto leg is a single escape cruise to the horizon (the Voyager/Pioneer pattern).

Pioneer 10 is **lower fidelity** — built on the DE118 Jupiter-flyby-era ephemeris; HORIZONS labels it "suitable for general historical purposes." Treat Voyager solutions as high-confidence; treat Pioneer 10 with caution.

## Element query

Request osculating elements with `EPHEM_TYPE='ELEMENTS'`, `REF_PLANE='ECLIPTIC'` (→ ecliptic of J2000, matching the `orbits.tsv` default plane), `REF_SYSTEM='ICRF'`, `OUT_UNITS='KM-S'`, `CENTER=<primary>`, `COMMAND='<id>'`, plus `START_TIME`/`STOP_TIME`/`STEP_SIZE`.

`CENTER` is the segment's gravitational primary, and **switching it is how patched-conic segments are produced** — query the *same* craft with `CENTER='@10'` (Sun, heliocentric cruise), `'@399'` (Earth, post-launch), `'@599'` (Jupiter flyby), etc., over successive time windows split at SOI crossings. Example (Voyager 1 heliocentric elements):

```
https://ssd.jpl.nasa.gov/api/horizons.api?format=text&COMMAND='-31'&EPHEM_TYPE='ELEMENTS'&CENTER='@10'&REF_PLANE='ECLIPTIC'&REF_SYSTEM='ICRF'&OUT_UNITS='KM-S'&START_TIME='1980-01-01'&STOP_TIME='1980-01-02'&STEP_SIZE='1d'
```

## HORIZONS ELEMENTS → `orbits.tsv` mapping

Verified against `program/table_orbit_builder.gd`. Write values in each column's declared unit (deg, km); the table importer converts to radians/internal units.

- `EC`→`eccentricity`, `IN`→`inclination`, `OM`→`longitude_ascending_node`, `W`→`argument_periapsis`; `parent` = the `CENTER` body (`STAR_SUN`, `PLANET_JUPITER`, …).
- **Size/phase:** use `semi_parameter` = `QR`·(1 + `EC`) [km] and `time_periapsis` from `Tp` for *every* segment — this open-conic form is valid at any eccentricity (the builder rejects `semi_major_axis`/`mean_anomaly_at_epoch`/`mean_motion` once e ≥ 1, which the hyperbolic flyby and escape legs are; the near-launch leg is mildly elliptic). HORIZONS returns `A` < 0 and `AD`/`PR` = 1e99 sentinels for hyperbolas — ignore them.
- `fix_gaps` = `x` on heliocentric **cruise** legs (`CENTER='@10'`); blank on flyby/departure anchors (see *Gap closing*).
- Leave `orbit_gravitational_parameter` blank — a segment inherits its parent body's GM.

## Gap closing, segmentation & the converter

A **flyby/departure** segment (`CENTER` ≠ `@10`) is a planet-centric osculating conic — an accurate anchor, and frame-portable (independent of where the planet is). A **cruise** segment (`CENTER='@10'`) is heliocentric and must terminate exactly at the flyby anchors' endpoints; authored from natural osculating elements it does not, leaving gaps. So cruises are flagged `fix_gaps`, and at **new-game** `IVTrajectory._fix_gaps` re-fits each flagged cruise as the conic (Lambert) through its neighbor segments' boundary positions, computed from the bodies' *actual* runtime positions. Continuity therefore survives a change of planet-orbit model (`IVRealPlanetOrbit` vs `IVOrbit`) or data source. It runs new-game only — the fixed orbits persist with the planet orbits they were fit to, so a load needs no re-fit (`fix_gaps` is transient and not persisted). Generic two-body math lives in `IVOrbit.solve_lambert` and `IVOrbit.create_from_state_and_precessions`.

**Earth-launched interplanetary craft start with a geocentric Earth departure segment** (`CENTER='@399'`, a `fix_gaps`-false anchor): it captures the launch escape hyperbola, makes the first cruise an interior two-anchor fit, and is visible when zoomed to Earth. The converter sets this segment's `segment_begin` to the conic's own `time_periapsis` — perigee is the hyperbola's nearest point to Earth (a few hundred km up, the synthetic departure), so the arc begins at the surface rather than tens of Earth-diameters up the incoming asymptote, where a bare launch-date `begin` would otherwise place it (hours before perigee). (Earth-orbiters like ISS/Hubble are plain orbits, not trajectories.)

`tools/horizons_trajectory.py` automates the per-segment HORIZONS queries and the mapping above; segmentation (each segment's primary and its SOI-crossing `begin`/`end`/`sample` window) is hand-maintained in the script's `CRAFT` dict. Run one craft with `python tools/horizons_trajectory.py <craft> --write` (`<craft>` ∈ `voyager_1`, `voyager_2`, `pioneer_10`, `juno`); drop `--write` for a dry run that only prints the rows. **Default:** natural osculating cruises + `fix_gaps`, so the engine closes the joins at runtime — HORIZONS queries only, no sim launch. **`--pre-fix`:** Lambert-fits the cruises offline against the running sim's planet positions, so shipped data is gap-free with no runtime fix (slower — launches the sim). `tools/verify_trajectory.py --launch` drives the sim to check each segment's primary and the join continuity. The writer maps row values to columns by header **name** (as the GDScript reader does), reading each table's column order from its header at run time — so a column reorder or insertion in a `.tsv` needs no change here, and a column it writes that's renamed or removed aborts with a message naming it rather than silently misaligning the row.

## Capture orbits and multi-revolution cruises

A captured craft (e.g. Juno) needs one new segment kind plus one segmentation rule beyond the cruise/flyby/departure set above.

**`visual_orbit` segments.** A planet-centric leg can be flagged to draw as a standalone local orbit (an ellipse in the body's own parent frame) rather than woven into the trajectory polyline. The flag is an optional 6th element on the segment tuple in the `CRAFT` config — `("JUPITER_CAPTURE", "@599", begin, end, sample, {"visual_orbit"})`. The converter collects the flagged indexes into the `visual_orbits` column of trajectories.tsv (`ARRAY[INT]`); `IVTrajectory.is_orbit_segment` reads it and `IVPathVisual` renders those segments in orbit mode, omitting them from the polyline. Use it for the capture orbit and later "representative" orbits. Their segment windows still tile contiguously, but the orbits are independent osculating snapshots — the body jumps between them at each boundary (we don't model the maneuvers/encounters that changed the orbit, so these joins are *expected* to be discontinuous; `verify_trajectory.py` reports them as info, not failures).

**Multi-revolution cruises — split, don't special-case.** A heliocentric leg whose true-anomaly sweep reaches ≳ 360° cannot be a single `fix_gaps` cruise: the re-fit is a *zero-revolution* Lambert solve (both the Python `lambert()` and `IVOrbit.solve_lambert`), which for a ≥ 1-rev time of flight silently returns a wrong sub-revolution conic rather than erroring. Split the arc into sub-revolution legs — at a mid-course maneuver if there is one, otherwise at any waypoint — and flag each `fix_gaps` as usual.

Consecutive `fix_gaps` cruises chain correctly, so the split needs no special handling. `IVTrajectory._fix_gaps` runs once at new-game, segment by segment in order, and `IVOrbit.create_from_state_and_precessions` anchors each re-fit cruise *exactly* at its `begin_target`. So for a run of cruises bounded by anchors A → c1 → c2 → … → B: c1 is fit from A (a real anchor) to c2's natural-osculating boundary point; c2 is then re-anchored to c1's *freshly-fixed* endpoint and fit on to c3's natural point; and so on, the last leg closing on B. Every interior join closes because each leg's start is pinned to where its predecessor actually ended — the natural-osculating position at each boundary is simply the glue point both adjacent legs are fit through. The one real constraint is per-leg, not per-chain: **keep each leg under ~1 revolution.** (Half-revolution legs are near-180° transfers — Lambert's geometrically degenerate case; `solve_lambert` / `create_from_state_and_precessions` carry it in double precision, robust in practice but the first place precision would erode for an almost-exactly-antipodal pair.)

**Juno** is the worked example: launch → a ~1-revolution heliocentric loop split at the 2012 deep-space maneuver into two sub-revolution `fix_gaps` cruises (`CRUISE_1A`/`1B`) → Earth gravity-assist flyby → a `fix_gaps` cruise to Jupiter's SOI vicinity → the pre-JOI approach hyperbola (`JUPITER_APPROACH`, a flyby-style anchor drawn in the polyline) swinging in to perijove → a 53.5-day polar capture orbit, then one `visual_orbit` per post-capture period era (53.5 → 43 → 38 → 33 d, boundaries at the Ganymede/Europa/Io flybys). The capture orbit and the approach hyperbola share that perijove (the JOI burn is impulsive there), so the polyline's swing-in meets the local capture ellipse. Its `begin` (start of life) is the departure `segment_begin`, and the final orbit's `segment_end` runs to 2100 so Juno keeps orbiting (Voyager convention).

## Time base & boundary precision

Internal sim time is **TT, J2000 epoch, in seconds** (`program/timekeeper.gd`; JD 2451545.0 = t₀). HORIZONS emits JDTDB; TT ≈ TDB to < 2 ms, so ignore the distinction. `segment_begin`, `segment_end`, and `time_periapsis` are sim seconds: `(JD − 2451545.0) × 86400`. The `epoch_jd` column instead holds a raw JD (the builder converts it). Simplest convention: leave `epoch_jd` blank (= J2000) and write `time_periapsis` as absolute J2000 seconds.

Segment boundary times come from the per-segment `begin`/`end` strings in the converter's `CRAFT` config, via `date_to_jd`. A bare `YYYY-MM-DD` pins the boundary to **midnight** (whole-day granularity); pass `YYYY-MM-DDTHH:MM:SS` for sub-day precision. Day granularity is invisible across a slow heliocentric join — adjacent segments share the *same* boundary value and `fix_gaps` re-fits the cruise to meet its neighbor at that shared instant, so the join closes regardless of the boundary's absolute accuracy — but it misplaces an **open terminal** (the departure start, which is why that begin is pinned to perigee) and any join in a **fast planet-moon frame**, where half a day is hundreds of thousands of km. Give those boundaries an explicit time.

**After editing any `.tsv`, refresh imports** (`<godot-console> --path . --import --headless`) before launching — headless runs use cached table data and silently ignore un-reimported edits. Godot keys reimport on the source's md5, so a regenerate that reproduces identical bytes is correctly a no-op (mtime is not consulted). Note `--editor --headless --quit` quits *before* the async import scan completes — use `--import`, which waits for imports then exits.

## Known imprecisions

The pipeline targets *visual* fidelity (patched conics tuned in-sim), not navigation-grade accuracy. Residuals worth knowing, roughly largest first:

- **HORIZONS solution fidelity** — Pioneer 10 is DE118-era (see *Targets*); the Voyagers are 2022 DE440 refits. This dominates absolute error.
- **TT vs UTC (~50 s in the 1977–89 launch/flyby era, ~69 s today; uncorrected)** — `date_to_jd` treats config calendar dates as TT with uniform days (no leap-second table). A real UTC encounter time transcribed verbatim lands that far off. Because boundaries are synthetic SOI crossings tuned in-sim, and `time_periapsis` comes straight from HORIZONS (already TDB ≈ TT), this shifts absolute *labeling*, not the join geometry — but don't expect a transcribed UTC instant to be second-exact.
- **Default day-granularity boundaries** — see *Time base & boundary precision*: self-consistent at interior joins, but matters at open terminals and in fast frames. Mitigated with an explicit `THH:MM:SS`.
- **Float formatting** — rows print with `%.12g`; at sim-time magnitudes (~1e9 s) that keeps ≳10 ms, and times routed through the JD intermediate carry ~5×10⁻⁵ s — both far finer than 1 s. The cap to be aware of, not a practical limit.
- **Departure perigee is synthetic** — the departure `segment_begin` is the osculating hyperbola's back-projected `time_periapsis`, close to but not exactly liftoff (by design — see the departure-segment note).
